Q0. Please answer the following questions in your own words.

1. What makes a model "linear"? "Linear" in what? 
What makes a model linear is if it is linear in terms of the coefficients. If the terms are only added together and multiplied by the coefficients, it is linear.
2. How do you interpret the coefficient for a dummy/one-hot-encoded variable? (This is a trick question, and the trick involves how you handle the intercept of the model.) There's further explanation at the end of this document, if needed.
A coefficient for a dummy variable is interpreted as the amount of variance an outcome is compared to the reference category or group (the reference being what was dropped). 
3. Can linear regression be used for classification? Explain why, or why not.
Linear regression could be used for classification, but it does not work the best because you could get values that are less than 0 or greater than 1 which is not a real probability. 
4. If you have a high accuracy on training but low on testing, what might be the problem? It also might be you see a pattern in the residuals.
High accuracy on training but low testing accuracy leads means overfitting is the problem. 
5. Review this page: Non-Linear with Linear What are two ways to incorporate nonlinear relationships between your target/response/dependent/outcome variable 
 and your features/control/response/independent variables 
?
Two ways to incorporate nonlinear relationships are transofrming features and adding polynomial terms. 
5. What is the interpretation of the intercept? A slope coefficient for a variable? The coefficient for a dummy/one-hot-encoded variable?
The intercept is the predicted value of y when the variables are equal to 0. The slope coefficient for a variable is the change in y for a one unit increase in the variable. The dummy variable coefficient is the difference in the predicted y between that category/group and the reference group.
